# 11. Behavioral & Cross-Functional Questions

These assess collaboration, judgment, and communication — critical for a role that sits at the intersection of engineering, neuroscience, clinical, and regulatory teams. Sample strong-answer guidance is given rather than a single scripted answer, since these should be answered with real personal experience.

---

### 11.1 Tell me about a time you had to make a technical tradeoff between performance/capability and safety. How did you approach it?

**What interviewers look for:** Evidence the candidate treats safety as a genuine engineering constraint to be reasoned about carefully, not an afterthought or box-checking exercise — and that they can articulate a clear, specific decision-making process (not just "we chose safety," but *how* they weighed the actual tradeoff, who was involved, and what evidence informed the decision).

---

### 11.2 Describe a situation where you had to explain a complex technical concept (e.g., a protocol design decision) to a non-technical stakeholder — a clinician, regulatory reviewer, or executive. How did you approach it?

**What interviewers look for:** Clear communication ability and genuine translation of technical substance (not oversimplification that loses important nuance), and ideally evidence the candidate sought to understand what the stakeholder actually needed to know for their own decision-making, rather than just explaining the topic in the abstract.

---

### 11.3 Tell me about a time a design you were confident in turned out to be wrong, or a bug made it further into development/testing than it should have. What happened, and what did you change afterward?

**What interviewers look for:** Genuine accountability and specific, concrete process improvements that resulted (better test coverage, a new review step, improved simulation fidelity) — this field requires comfort with rigorous post-incident learning, not defensiveness or vague "we learned to be more careful" answers.

---

### 11.4 How do you approach working with a cross-functional team that includes neuroscientists, clinicians, and regulatory affairs specialists who may not share your engineering background or vocabulary?

**What interviewers look for:** Evidence of genuine collaborative curiosity (asking questions to understand the clinical/scientific "why" behind a requirement, not just implementing a spec handed down) and respect for the expertise other disciplines bring — this role fails if an engineer treats clinical/regulatory input as an obstacle rather than essential design input.

---

### 11.5 Describe a time you had to push back on a stakeholder (a manager, a clinical partner, a regulatory requirement) because you believed a proposed approach was unsafe or technically unsound. How did you handle that conversation?

**What interviewers look for:** Willingness to advocate for safety/technical correctness even under pressure, combined with professional, evidence-based communication rather than pure obstruction — strong candidates describe bringing data/analysis to support their position and finding a collaborative path forward, not just refusing.

---

### 11.6 Tell me about a project where requirements were ambiguous or changed significantly partway through. How did you adapt?

**What interviewers look for:** Comfort with ambiguity balanced against appropriate rigor for a safety-critical domain — a strong answer shows the candidate could adapt without cutting corners on validation/testing, and ideally shows they helped drive clarity (e.g., by proposing a phased approach or explicitly documenting assumptions) rather than just passively absorbing churn.

---

### 11.7 Have you ever had to say "no" to a feature request or timeline because it genuinely couldn't be done safely or correctly in the available time? How did you communicate that?

**What interviewers look for:** Evidence the candidate can hold a firm line on safety/quality without simply being obstructionist — ideally describing how they proposed an alternative (a reduced scope, a phased approach, a longer timeline with clear justification) rather than just delivering a flat refusal.

---

### 11.8 Describe your experience (or how you'd approach it, if you don't have direct experience) working within a regulated, documentation-heavy development process, compared to a faster-moving, less formal software environment.

**What interviewers look for:** Realistic understanding that this field requires more process rigor than much of the tech industry, and genuine (not merely tolerant) appreciation for why that rigor exists — candidates who clearly resent or want to minimize process/documentation work are often a poor fit for this specific domain, regardless of their raw technical skill.

---

### 11.9 Tell me about a time you had to debug a difficult, intermittent hardware/firmware issue. Walk me through your process.

**What interviewers look for:** Systematic debugging methodology (forming hypotheses, designing tests to isolate variables, using data/logging rather than guessing) — particularly relevant given how much of this role involves exactly this kind of difficult embedded/RF debugging, often without the benefit of easy reproducibility.

---

### 11.10 How do you personally stay current with developments in neural interface technology, given how fast the underlying neuroscience and engineering are evolving?

**What interviewers look for:** Genuine engagement with the field (specific papers, conferences, open questions they find interesting) rather than a generic answer — this role benefits from real intellectual curiosity about the neuroscience, not just the engineering, given how much protocol/system design decisions depend on understanding the underlying signals and clinical context.

---

### 11.11 Describe a time you had to balance moving quickly (to meet a milestone, hit a trial deadline, etc.) against wanting to be more thorough. How did you decide where to draw the line?

**What interviewers look for:** Evidence of risk-based thinking — a strong candidate describes distinguishing between areas where cutting scope/time was acceptable (lower-risk functionality) versus areas where they held firm on thoroughness regardless of schedule pressure (safety-critical paths), echoing the risk-proportionate rigor theme throughout this repo's technical categories.

---

### 11.12 Tell me about a time you disagreed with a colleague's technical approach. How did you resolve it?

**What interviewers look for:** Collaborative, evidence-based conflict resolution (proposing experiments/data to settle the disagreement, being genuinely open to being wrong) rather than either capitulating without real conviction or being unable to move past disagreement productively.

---

### 11.13 What draws you specifically to neural interface technology, as opposed to other areas of embedded systems or protocol design you could work in?

**What interviewers look for:** Authentic motivation and some evidence of having thought seriously about both the technical challenge and the human/clinical stakes involved — candidates who can articulate genuine interest in the intersection of engineering and improving patients' lives (without it sounding rehearsed) tend to be a stronger long-term fit for a field that requires sustained patience through slow, heavily-validated development cycles.

---

### 11.14 Tell me about a time you had to make a decision with incomplete information, in a context where getting it wrong could have serious consequences. How did you approach it?

**What interviewers look for:** A structured approach to decision-making under uncertainty (explicitly reasoning about what's known vs. unknown, seeking the most valuable additional information given time constraints, building in appropriate safety margin for the uncertainty that remains) rather than either false confidence or paralysis.

---

### 11.15 How would you approach onboarding onto a legacy codebase/protocol you didn't design, especially one running in devices already implanted in patients, where you need to become confident enough to make changes safely?

**What interviewers look for:** Appropriate humility and rigor when working with an unfamiliar safety-critical system — a strong answer describes wanting to thoroughly understand the existing risk analysis/FMEA documentation, test coverage, and design rationale before making changes, rather than assuming they can safely modify code based on reading it alone, and describes a deliberate process for building confidence (extensive testing in simulation before touching anything that affects real devices) rather than rushing to contribute.
